Empathy in humans towards a robot

Evoking an empathetic response in humans while interacting with a robot

Summary

This project explores human empathy toward robots by creating a scenario where participants encourage a robot to cross a dangerous bridge. Using physiological measurements like heart rate and skin conductance, it analyzes how participants emotionally respond to the robot's challenges. Results showed heightened emotional arousal when the robot encountered difficulties or failed, and relief when it succeeded. The study emphasizes that people can form empathetic bonds with robots, as seen through their emotional engagement, providing insights into human-robot interaction dynamics.

Flow of the experiment

Step 1: Robot greets participant
Purpose: To initiate interaction and establish a connection with the participant.

Step 2: Participant asks the robot to cross the bridge
Interaction begins, and the participant introduces the task for the robot.

Step 3: Robot hesitates to cross the bridge
Introducing an element of hesitation creates a challenge.

Step 4: Participant encourages the robot
Encouragement serves as a motivational intervention for the robot.

Step 5: Robot attempts to cross the bridge
The first attempt at completing the task.

Step 6: Scary robot appears and causes the robot to fall
Introducing a new challenge to increase the difficulty.

Step 7: Participant encourages the robot again
The participant's continued support is tested after the failure.

Step 8: Robot successfully crosses the bridge
Conclusion of the task, with the robot completing the challenge.

Analyze the result

Fig. 1. Tonic EDA in 1 ~ 8 step of the flow

Fig. 2. Tonic EDA in 9 ~ 13 step of the flow

There was no prominent tendency during the 1 ~ 8 steps of the experiment. Two participants (participant 1 and participant 5) showed higher Tonic EDA levels at the end of the experiment compared to the beginning. Meanwhile, the rest of the participants did not show a consistent tendency. In contrast, during steps 9 to 13, we could observe that five participants out of six showed gradually increasing Tonic EDA.

Fig. 3. Power Spectral Density for frequency domains of heart rate variability in 1 ~ 8 step of the flow

Fig. 4. Power Spectral Density for frequency domains of heart rate variability in 9 ~ 13 step of the flow

Akin to Tonic EDA, participants showed high frequency after the robot vector had fallen off the bridge. In the early stages of the experiment, participants mainly showed low frequency in heart rate; however, this tendency completely changed after entering step 9.

As a result, we could conclude that participants were emotionally aroused in the first stage of the experiment (commanding the robots to do what the robot hesitated to do). This might be caused by commanding the robot by opposing its will or by observing the robot fall off the bridge. When the robot finally completes its task, the emotional arousal decreases significantly. From this experiment, we could observe physiological changes in participants according to the robot's behaviors.
