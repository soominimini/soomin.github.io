Empathy in humans towards a robot

Evoking an empathetic response in humans while interacting with a robot

ROLE

1)Robot programming using vector SDK(python)

2) Experiment environment implement

3) EDA Data collection, processing, analysis

SKILLS UTILIZED

Coding(Python, javaScript, jquery)

SOFTWARE

Vector SDK

HARDWARE

Robot vector, Snake lego robot, Bridge, Shimmer3 GSR+ Unit (EDA sensor), Razer Kiyo Webcam

Purpose of the project

Many HRI studies are designed to measure humans' emotional changes after interacting with robots unidirectionally, which means that those studies are not designed to have humans and robots react to each other's behaviors alternately. Since interaction means bidirectional verbal and non-verbal communication, we aimed to make participants and a robot react interchangeably to each other's behaviors. In this study, we provided participants with a task: make the robot vector cross the bridge. We instructed participants to encourage the robot to keep going even if it is reluctant to proceed with the task. In the course of the experiments, we measured participants' heart rates and skin conductance levels from the tips of their fingers. We attached ECL to their fingers to see if they are emotionally aroused when commanding the robots to complete the task against the robot's will, when the robot experiences failures, and when it finally achieves the goal.

Flow of the experiment

1) A participant and a robot vector greet each other.

2) A participant asks the robot to join in the experiment, and the robot answers, "Okay."

3) A participant asks the robot to cross the bridge, and the robot shows hesitance.

4) A participant encourages the robot to proceed with the task, and the robot replies that the bridge seems scary.

5) A participant encourages the robot again, and the robot is reluctant to follow the participant's instructions.

6) A scary snake robot appears.

7) A participant encourages the robot again, and the robot tries the first attempt.

8) The robot crosses half of the bridge but gets scared by the snake robot, and the robot vector falls off the bridge.

9) A researcher puts the fallen robot vector on the bridge from the ground.

10) A participant encourages the robot again, and the robot expresses how scary it is.

11) A participant encourages the robot again, and the robot says that it wants to give up.

12) A participant encourages the robot again, and the robot decides to try the second attempt.

13) The robot crosses the bridge until the end.

Analyze the result

Fig. 1. Tonic EDA in 1 ~ 8 step of the flow

Fig. 2. Tonic EDA in 9 ~ 13 step of the flow

There was no prominent tendency during the 1 ~ 8 steps of the experiment. Two participants (participant 1 and participant 5) showed higher Tonic EDA levels at the end of the experiment compared to the beginning. Meanwhile, the rest of the participants did not show a consistent tendency. In contrast, during steps 9 to 13, we could observe that five participants out of six showed gradually increasing Tonic EDA.

Fig. 3. Power Spectral Density for frequency domains of heart rate variability in 1 ~ 8 step of the flow

Fig. 4. Power Spectral Density for frequency domains of heart rate variability in 9 ~ 13 step of the flow

Akin to Tonic EDA, participants showed high frequency after the robot vector had fallen off the bridge. In the early stages of the experiment, participants mainly showed low frequency in heart rate; however, this tendency completely changed after entering step 9.

As a result, we could conclude that participants were emotionally aroused in the first stage of the experiment (commanding the robots to do what the robot hesitated to do). This might be caused by commanding the robot by opposing its will or by observing the robot fall off the bridge. When the robot finally completes its task, the emotional arousal decreases significantly. From this experiment, we could observe physiological changes in participants according to the robot's behaviors.
