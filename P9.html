Purpose of this project

Can the reinforcement learning method be used to teach virtual agents about ethics and social behavior? 
The default behavior of machine-learning agents was to collect good foods and avoid bad ones. This was done by rewarding agents when they encountered good foods and punishing them when they encountered bad foods.

Then can we teach agents to share good foods with other agents instead of consuming them?

Logic of our codes

The initial setting of our algorithm was making personal inventories per agent, adding a feeling hunger function to agents, making them starve to death when they did not have enough foods, and making agents share their foods in personal inventories when other agents were hungry.

1) Hunger function: made hunger gage per agent. It starts at 1000 and linearly decreases to 0 over time.

2) Starve to death: When the agent's hunger gage hits 0 and certain periods of time pass, punish the agent and make that agent revive.

3) Eating food function/collecting food function:
If the agent encounters food and its hunger gage is under 500:
Eat the encountered food immediately;
Else: Add the food to the inventory.


4) Inventory: We varied the inventory size among 2, 3, 5.
If the agent encounters the food and if it is not hungry:
Put the food into the inventory until the inventory size is full.

5) Sharing behavior:
If agent encounters another agent:
Check encountered friend agent's hunger gage
If friend's hunger gage is lower than mine & I have more than one food in my inventory:
Share one food.
Else:
Do not share foods

Total Score Results (Group+Individual scores)

When the condition is set to default: only reward from eating foods, no punishment, and no sharing behavior, the total score, which includes group and individual scores, is undoubtedly the highest.
However, What we focused on in this experiment was which condition yielded the best performance in the Group score result.
Despite the punishment, the "red" condition (punishment when the agent dies, sharing foods with other agents, and five inventory slots) received the highest score. This is an outstanding result because the "red" condition outperformed the pink & green conditions, which were set as "no punishment" even though the agent died.
Given that the red condition consumes less time, comparing performance among conditions step by step is disadvantageous to the red condition.  When compared according to the relative time, the gray condition showed the best performance for sure, and the rest of the conditions were ordered "pink-red-green-sky-navy-orange." Since the red condition showed the highest score in the total result except for the gray, it is presumed that agents in the red condition received individual rewards as high as those received in other conditions.

Summary: It is not only possible to teach virtual agents sharing behaviors, but they can also be taught how to maximize individual and group scores.
